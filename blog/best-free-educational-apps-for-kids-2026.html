1. Best Free Reading Apps

Reading is the foundation of everything. If your kid can read well, everything else gets easier. Here are the apps that actually help.

Khan Academy Kids (Ages 2-8)

This is the gold standard and I'll say it right up front. Khan Academy Kids is 100% free. No ads. No subscriptions. No "premium tier" that locks out the good stuff. It covers reading, phonics, math, social-emotional learning, and more.

My 4-year-old went from recognizing about 10 letters to reading simple three-letter words in six weeks using this app. The characters are cute without being annoying, and the lessons build on each other in a way that makes sense.

What makes it special is the adaptive learning path. The app figures out where your kid is and adjusts. No setup needed from you beyond creating a free account.

Epic! Free Library (Ages 4-12)

Epic gives kids access to over 40,000 books. The free version gives you one book per day, which honestly is enough for most families. If your kid is a reading machine, the paid version is about $10/month, but start with free and see how it goes.

The selection is genuinely impressive. We found everything from early readers to chapter books to nonfiction about dinosaurs and space. The audiobook feature is great for car rides.

Starfall (Ages Pre-K to 3rd Grade)

Starfall has been around forever and there's a reason for that. The free version covers phonics and basic reading skills really well. The website version actually has more free content than the app, so try both.

My 7-year-old still goes back to Starfall for the reading activities even though she's technically past the target age. That tells you something about how engaging it is.

Want to make reading time more comfortable? A solid tablet stand for kids keeps the screen at the right angle and saves you from holding it during storytime.

2. Best Free Math Apps

Math apps are tricky because a lot of them are basically digital flashcards with cartoon characters. The good ones actually teach mathematical thinking, not just memorization.

Prodigy Math (Ages 6-14)

Prodigy turns math into an RPG game. Your kid creates a character, goes on quests, and has to solve math problems to progress. The core math content is completely free. The paid membership just gives access to extra cosmetic items and pets.

Here's the thing I love about Prodigy: my 10-year-old doesn't realize he's doing math. He thinks he's playing a video game. He voluntarily does math problems on weekends now. I never thought I'd type that sentence.

The curriculum aligns with actual school standards from 1st through 8th grade, so what your kid learns here transfers directly to the classroom.

Khan Academy (Ages 8+)

For older kids, regular Khan Academy is unbeatable for math. From basic arithmetic all the way through calculus, every lesson is free. The practice problems adapt to your kid's level, and the video explanations are clear and patient.

I started my 10-year-old on the 4th grade math section and he's already moved ahead to some 5th grade concepts. The progress tracking is helpful for parents too -- you can see exactly what your kid has mastered.

Moose Math by Duck Duck Moose (Ages 3-7)

For the little ones, Moose Math makes counting, addition, subtraction, and basic geometry feel like play. It's completely free and ad-free. The activities are genuinely fun, not just worksheets on a screen.

My 4-year-old's favorite is the smoothie shop game where she has to count ingredients. She asks to play it every day.

3. Best Free Science Apps

Science apps are where things get really cool because you can show kids things that are impossible to show in real life.

NASA App (All Ages)

The official NASA app is free and packed with real images from space, live tracking of the International Space Station, and educational content about missions past and present. It's not gamified, but for kids who are even slightly interested in space, it's magical.

We use the ISS tracker to go outside and actually watch the space station fly overhead. Seeing it in real life after tracking it on the app is the kind of moment that makes a kid fall in love with science.

Tinybop Explorer's Library (Ages 4-10)

Tinybop makes beautifully designed science apps. The Human Body app is often available for free, and it lets kids explore different body systems by interacting with them. Feed the character food and watch the digestive system work. Make them run and see the heart beat faster.

The art style is gorgeous and the interactions are thoughtful. This is what educational apps should look like.

Google Arts & Culture (Ages 8+)

Not strictly a science app, but the science content here is incredible. Virtual tours of museums, high-resolution images of artifacts, and interactive experiments. All free, all fascinating.

My oldest spent an entire Saturday exploring the Natural History Museum in London through this app. No plane ticket required.

If your kids are getting into science experiments at home, having a decent kids science experiment kit alongside these apps takes learning to a whole new level.

4. Best Free Coding Apps for Kids

Coding is one of those skills where free resources are often better than paid ones. Here's what actually works.

ScratchJr (Ages 5-7) and Scratch (Ages 8-16)

Made by MIT, Scratch is the most popular coding platform for kids in the world, and it's completely free. ScratchJr is the simplified version for younger kids. Both use visual block-based coding where kids snap together pieces like a puzzle to make characters move, tell stories, and create games.

My 7-year-old made her first animated story in ScratchJr within an hour. My 10-year-old has built actual playable games in Scratch. The community is huge, so kids can share their projects and remix other people's work.

Code.org (Ages 4-18)

Code.org offers structured coding courses for every age group, all free. The Hour of Code activities are a great starting point -- themed around Minecraft, Star Wars, Frozen, and other stuff kids already love.

For older kids, the Computer Science Discoveries and Principles courses are genuinely rigorous. Some schools use Code.org as their official CS curriculum.

Swift Playgrounds (Ages 10+, iPad/Mac only)

If your family is in the Apple ecosystem, Swift Playgrounds teaches real Swift programming through puzzles and challenges. It's made by Apple and it's free. The production quality is incredible and kids end up learning an actual programming language used to build real apps.

5. Best Free Language Learning Apps

Duolingo (Ages 6+)

You probably already know Duolingo, but it's worth mentioning because the free version is genuinely powerful. Over 40 languages, gamified lessons, and a streak system that keeps kids (and adults) coming back daily.

My 10-year-old has been learning Spanish on Duolingo for about a year. He can hold a basic conversation now. The free version has ads between lessons, but they're short and not inappropriate.

Duolingo ABC (Ages 3-7)

Separate from regular Duolingo, this app focuses on English reading and writing skills. It's 100% free with no ads. The lessons are bite-sized and the progression is smooth. Great for preschoolers learning their letters.

6. Best Free Creative Learning Apps

GarageBand (Free on Apple devices)

GarageBand is a professional-grade music creation tool and it's free on every Apple device. Kids can learn about rhythm, melody, and composition by actually making music. The touch instruments make it accessible even for little kids.

We've had entire family jam sessions using GarageBand on different devices. It's a blast.

Canva for Education (Free)

Canva's education version gives students and teachers free access to premium features. Kids can create presentations, posters, videos, and more. It teaches design thinking and digital literacy in a practical way.

Tinkercad (Ages 8+)

Made by Autodesk, Tinkercad is a free browser-based 3D design tool. Kids can design objects, learn basic engineering concepts, and even create things to 3D print. The learning curve is gentle and there are tons of guided tutorials.

If your kid gets into 3D design on Tinkercad, a beginner 3D printer lets them hold what they create in their hands. That connection between digital and physical is powerful.

7. How to Manage Screen Time with Educational Apps

Here's something nobody talks about in these app recommendation posts: even educational screen time needs limits. Here's how our family handles it.

The 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. We set a timer and make it a game.

Active vs. passive: Coding on Scratch is active learning. Watching someone else play a game is passive. We weight active screen time differently than passive.

Mix it up: Our rule is that every 30 minutes of app time needs to be followed by 15 minutes of something physical. Jump rope, dance party, whatever gets them moving.

Set time limits by age:

  • Ages 2-5: 1 hour per day total screen time (including non-educational)
  • Ages 6-10: 1.5-2 hours of educational app time
  • Ages 11+: More flexible, but still with breaks built in

Use built-in parental controls: Both iOS (Screen Time) and Android (Family Link) let you set app time limits, content restrictions, and bedtime schedules. Use them. They're free and they work.

9. Red Flags: Apps to Avoid

While we're here, let me save you some headaches. Watch out for these warning signs.

"Free" apps that lock content after level 3. If an app lets your kid play the first few levels for free and then demands $9.99/month to continue, it's not a free app. It's a demo. ABCmouse and Homer are the biggest offenders here.

Apps with manipulative reward systems. If the app gives your kid coins, gems, or currencies that they can "buy" things with, but then runs out and pushes in-app purchases -- that's not education, that's a casino for children. Check the App Store reviews for mentions of aggressive monetization.

Apps with social features and no moderation. Any app that lets your kid chat with strangers without robust moderation is a no-go. Check the privacy policy and parental controls before letting your kid near social features.

Apps that collect too much data. Check what permissions the app asks for. A reading app doesn't need access to your camera, microphone, and location. Use Common Sense Media (commonsensemedia.org) to check privacy ratings.

Apps with excessive ads. A few short ads between lessons? Fine. Full-screen video ads every 2 minutes with a tiny close button designed to trick kids into clicking? Delete immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are free educational apps as good as paid ones?

In many cases, yes. Khan Academy Kids (free) is widely considered better than ABCmouse (paid). The key is looking at who makes the app and why. Nonprofits and universities tend to make the best free options because their goal is education, not profit. Paid apps need to keep you subscribing, so they sometimes prioritize engagement over actual learning.

How much screen time should my kid have with educational apps?

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends no more than 1 hour of screen time for kids ages 2-5, with more flexibility for older kids. For educational apps specifically, most experts say 30-60 minutes per session with breaks is ideal. The quality of the app matters more than the exact minutes. Active learning (coding, problem solving) is different from passive watching.

My kid only wants to play games, not use educational apps. What do I do?

Start with apps that feel like games. Prodigy Math is literally an RPG. Scratch lets kids make their own games. The trick is not framing it as "educational time" vs. "fun time." Just put Prodigy next to their regular games and let them discover it. Most kids get hooked once they try it because the game mechanics are genuinely good.

Do these apps work offline?

Some do. Khan Academy Kids has an offline mode where you can download lessons. Epic! lets you save books for offline reading. Most others require an internet connection. If you need offline access (road trips, areas with spotty wifi), Khan Academy Kids and Epic! are your best bets. Check each app's settings for download or offline options.

Are these apps safe for kids in terms of privacy?

The apps on this list are all from reputable companies that comply with COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act). Khan Academy, Scratch (MIT), and Code.org are nonprofits with strong privacy records. For any app you download, check Common Sense Media's privacy reviews. Also make sure you're the one creating the account, not your child, so you control what information is shared.

What tablet is best for kids' educational apps?

You don't need an expensive tablet. An Amazon Fire Kids tablet (around $60-100) runs most educational apps and comes with a kid-proof case and 2-year replacement guarantee. An older iPad works great too. The key is getting a sturdy kid-proof tablet case and setting up parental controls before handing it over.

Can educational apps replace tutoring?

For many kids, yes. Khan Academy's math program is used by schools as their primary curriculum. If your kid is struggling with a specific subject and you can't afford a tutor, these apps provide structured, adaptive instruction that adjusts to their level. They won't replace the human connection of a great teacher, but they're a solid alternative when tutoring isn't an option.

The Bottom Line

You don't need to spend money to give your kids a great educational app experience. The best apps in 2026 are either completely free (Khan Academy Kids, Scratch, Code.org) or have free tiers that are genuinely useful (Prodigy, Epic!, Duolingo).

Start with one or two apps that match your kid's age and interests. Don't overwhelm them with five apps on day one. Let them explore, get comfortable, and build a routine. You'll be surprised how quickly they start choosing the educational app over the mindless games.

And remember -- these apps work best as a supplement to real-world experiences, not a replacement. Read physical books too. Do science experiments in the kitchen. Build things with your hands. The apps are a tool, not the whole toolbox.
